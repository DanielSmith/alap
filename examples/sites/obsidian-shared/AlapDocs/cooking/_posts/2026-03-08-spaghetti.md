## From Persia to Sicily

Spaghetti traces its roots to an ancient tradition of thin,
unleavened bread from the Middle East. Under the Sasanian Empire,
this bread was called *rishta* when cut into strips and dried for
preservation. When Arabs conquered Persia in the 7th century, they
spread the dried-pasta practice throughout their territories,
including Sicily from the 9th century onward, where the name
*itriyya* entered local languages.

By the 12th century, Sicily had become a major producer of dried
pasta for both local consumption and export markets. Italian
cookbooks began featuring pasta recipes, and in the 15th century,
Martino da Como provided a precise description of how "Sicilian
macaroni" was made: dough was stretched thin, cut with wire "as thin
as spagho (string)," and dried in the sun for approximately twelve
days during summer months.
