Carbonara is one of those dishes everyone has an opinion about. The
history is recent and the canonical recipe is contested.



## Origin

The true origins of carbonara remain obscure, though most sources
trace the dish to Rome's Lazio region. The dish emerged in its
modern form during the mid-twentieth century, with the name appearing
in print only after World War II. Historical evidence suggests
carbonara developed after the 1944 Allied liberation of Rome,
possibly evolving from a simple dish of bacon and eggs served over
spaghetti that American and British soldiers requested at Italian
establishments. The first documented recipe appeared in a 1952
Chicago cookbook, preceding any published Italian version by two
years.

## What goes in

Authentic carbonara relies on a specific set of ingredients that
define the classic preparation. The dish combines pasta, traditionally
spaghetti, with guanciale (cured pork jowl), pecorino romano cheese,
eggs, salt, and black pepper. While guanciale remains the traditional
meat choice in Italy, variations using pancetta or bacon appear
elsewhere. The sauce develops its characteristic creamy texture
through the gentle heating of raw eggs mixed with grated cheese and
pasta water — not through the addition of cream, which only became
common outside Italy in later decades.
