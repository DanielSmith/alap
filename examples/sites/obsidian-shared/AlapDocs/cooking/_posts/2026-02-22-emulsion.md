The hardest part of fixing a broken sauce is admitting it broke. Here's
why they do.

## What an emulsion is

An emulsion is a mixture of two liquids that normally don't blend
together, like oil and water. One liquid gets broken into tiny
droplets and scattered throughout the other liquid. Common examples
include mayonnaise, salad dressings, and milk. The key ingredient
that makes this possible is something called an *emulsifier*, which
acts like a peacemaker between the two liquids by reducing the
tension at their boundary.

## Why they break

Emulsions tend to be unstable because the two liquids naturally
want to separate and return to their original state. Over time, the
tiny droplets bump into each other, combine into larger droplets,
and eventually the mixture breaks apart. This is why vinaigrette
needs constant shaking before you use it — without that energy
input, the oil and vinegar quickly go their separate ways. Some
emulsions, like homogenized milk, can stay mixed longer because they
contain special stabilizers that help keep everything in place.
