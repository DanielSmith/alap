The Goldbergs are a different listening discipline from the WTC.
Where the Well-Tempered Clavier is a tour through every key, the
Goldbergs sit with one bass line and stretch it through thirty
variations.

## The legend

According to the historical account by Bach biographer Johann
Nikolaus Forkel, the variations were commissioned by Count
Kaiserling, a Russian ambassador suffering from insomnia. The Count
reportedly asked Bach to compose keyboard pieces that would "cheer
up" his sleepless nights. Bach accepted the challenge and created
thirty variations on a bass line, completing what became the only
work of its kind from his hand. Scholars have questioned Forkel's
account, written more than sixty years after the events,
particularly since the published score lacks any dedication to the
Count.

## The architecture

The work's ingenious architecture centers on a repeating bass line
and harmonic progression rather than a melodic theme. Every third
variation is a canon, ascending in complexity from unison through
the ninth, creating a mathematical pattern within the thirty
variations. Between these canons, Bach inserted contrasting pieces
— dances, toccatas, and ornate arias — establishing what
musicologist Ralph Kirkpatrick identified as a ternary structure
of canon, genre piece, and arabesque repeated nine times until the
final Quodlibet breaks the cycle.

## My current listening

The Glenn Gould 1981 recording is famous for a reason — and the
1955 one is famous for a different reason. I'm currently going
back and forth between Hewitt's harpsichord version and Perahia's
piano. Different instruments, different works almost.
