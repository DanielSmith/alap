## A short history of birding

The earliest recorded interest in observing birds for their beauty
rather than utility traces back to the late eighteenth century, when
naturalists began studying living birds rather than collected
specimens. This aesthetic appreciation gradually transformed into an
organized hobby, particularly after Edmund Selous published *Bird
Watching* in 1901, establishing the term itself. The twentieth
century saw birding expand dramatically across North America and
Europe, driven by improved optics and the publication of field
identification guides that made bird study accessible without
needing to shoot specimens.

Modern birders typically carry binoculars and a spotting scope,
along with field guides for identifying species by sight and sound.
Sound recognition has become equally important to visual
identification — the term *birding* is preferred over
*birdwatching* by many enthusiasts partly because it includes the
auditory aspects of enjoying birds.
