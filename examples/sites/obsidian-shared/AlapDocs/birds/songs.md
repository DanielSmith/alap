Most of what's identifiable in the spring chorus is song, not call — the
distinction is functional rather than acoustic.

> [!note]
> Songs tend to be longer, more intricate vocalizations tied to territorial
> defense and courtship, while calls serve simpler functions like maintaining
> flock cohesion or sounding alarms. Songs are longer and more complex and
> are associated with territory and courtship and mating; calls tend to
> serve such functions as alarms or keeping members of a flock in contact.

Birds have evolved to sing for compelling biological reasons, primarily
centered on reproduction and survival. The quality and complexity of a
male's song can indicate his overall fitness to potential mates, making
song an important factor in sexual selection. Established males also use
singing to defend their territories from rivals — the elaborate nature of
the vocalization sometimes lets them avoid physical confrontations by
demonstrating strength through sound alone.

## What I'm trying to learn this season

Three this spring, in order of how often I'm hearing them:

- The cardinal's *cheer-cheer-cheer* — easy.
- The wren's *teakettle* — once, but unmistakable.
- The robin's quiet caroling at dawn — harder than it should be.

> [!tip]
> Field guides describe song as a "phrase pattern" but the way you actually
> learn one is to hear it three or four times and finally have the brain
> admit it's the same bird. Don't push.
