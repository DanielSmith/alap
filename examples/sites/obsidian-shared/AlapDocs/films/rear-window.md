# Rear Window

> [!tip]
> The most accessible Hitchcock for someone new to his work. A
> real-time-feeling thriller built almost entirely from one set
> and one apartment window.
## Premise

Photojournalist Jeff Jefferies, confined to a wheelchair with a
broken leg, spends his recovery observing the lives of his
neighbors through his Greenwich Village apartment window. When he
witnesses suspicious behavior from a costume jewelry salesman,
Jeff becomes convinced the man has murdered his wife, leading him
to investigate from his immobilized vantage point with help from
his girlfriend Lisa and nurse Stella.

> [!note]- How the single-set construction works
> The film was shot entirely on a single soundstage at Paramount
> Studios, featuring an enormous indoor set replicating a
> Greenwich Village courtyard. Set designers spent six weeks
> constructing this complex, detailed environment with
> sophisticated lighting systems capable of creating natural
> effects for both day and night scenes. This singular setting
> allowed Hitchcock to maintain an intimate, claustrophobic
> perspective throughout the film, reinforcing the viewer's
> identification with Jeff's restricted point of view.
## Why it holds up

The voyeurism question gets posed and never fully resolved. Jeff is
a journalist by trade, used to observing — but his observation here
slides toward something less neutral, and the film keeps reminding
us he's watching strangers without their consent. Lisa joins him
in the watching; the audience joins them. By the end you've spent
two hours doing exactly what the film keeps suggesting is
questionable.

> [!tip] Watching tip
> The film never cuts to anything Jeff can't see from his window.
> Once you notice this, the discipline of it is the show.
