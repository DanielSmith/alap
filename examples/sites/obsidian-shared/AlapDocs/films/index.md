# A guide to Hitchcock

A personal walkthrough of selected Alfred Hitchcock films — what to
watch first, what to know going in, what each does that's worth
paying attention to.


> [!note]
> These notes lean on the films' formal qualities rather than
> plot summaries. Spoilers are flagged in dedicated callouts where
> they appear; otherwise the writing assumes you haven't seen the
> film yet.
## Style

Hitchcock distinguished himself through meticulous technical control
and innovative visual storytelling. He famously shot only what
appeared in the script, refusing to create additional footage that
could be reworked later — a method that sometimes frustrated
producer David Selznick. His films showcase careful composition and
editing, exemplified by techniques like placing a light inside a
glass of milk in *Suspicion* to direct audience attention, or
constructing a glass floor in *The Lodger* to capture unique
overhead perspectives that enhanced the psychological tension of
his narratives.

Throughout his career, Hitchcock cultivated his personal brand with
remarkable strategic awareness, understanding that the name of the
director should stay clearly in the mind of the audience. This
philosophy drove his cameo appearances, television hosting duties,
and self-promotional efforts beginning in the 1920s. By positioning
himself as the architect of suspense rather than merely a
facilitator of star performances, Hitchcock transformed the
director's role into something central to filmmaking itself,
establishing a template for auteur-driven cinema that continues
influencing filmmakers today.

## Where to start

- [[vertigo|Vertigo]] — 1958. The technical peak.
- [[rear-window|Rear Window]] — 1954. The most fun.
- [[psycho|Psycho]] — 1960. The most influential.
