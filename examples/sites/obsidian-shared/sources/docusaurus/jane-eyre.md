## First-person and innovation

Charlotte Brontë's novel employs an intimate first-person
perspective that was revolutionary for its time. The narrator, Jane
herself, recounts her experiences from childhood through adulthood,
allowing readers to experience her emotional and moral development
alongside her. This narrative technique creates what literary
critic Daniel S. Burt described as making Brontë "the first
historian of the private consciousness," establishing a
psychological intensity that influenced later modernist writers.

## Independence and class

Jane's journey reflects themes of independence and social mobility
that challenged Victorian conventions. As an orphaned governess
without wealth or family connections, she navigates the rigid
class hierarchy of early nineteenth-century England. Her refusal
to compromise her principles — notably when she rejects Rochester's
proposal to live as his mistress despite loving him —
demonstrates her determination to maintain personal integrity.
Through her inheritance and her eventual marriage as an equal
partner, Jane achieves both financial independence and emotional
fulfillment, embodying a form of feminism that was strikingly
progressive for 1847.
