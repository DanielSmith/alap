The cardinals are the most reliable visitors at the feeder. Year-round
residents — the bird at the feeder in February is the same one from last
August.

{{< note >}}
**Field marks.** The male northern cardinal is unmistakable with brilliant
crimson plumage and a distinctive black face mask. The female displays
more subtle fawn and grayish-brown tones with reddish highlights on the
wings and tail. Both sexes have prominent raised crests and striking bright
coral-colored beaks.
{{< /note >}}

The species shows interesting color variation. Plumage colour comes from
carotenoid pigments in their diet — males fed only yellow pigments become
a pale red instead of the typical vibrant scarlet you'd expect.

{{% heading "Song and territory" %}}

Cardinals are highly territorial, with males singing loud, clear whistles
from elevated perches to defend their domain. The vocalizations are
distinctive and regionally variable — both sexes sing patterns that are
repeated and then varied.

{{< tip >}}
During winter months, cardinals fluff their down feathers to retain warmth
and can even lower their body temperature by several degrees to survive
cold weather. They're year-round residents in their range, not migrants.
{{< /tip >}}
