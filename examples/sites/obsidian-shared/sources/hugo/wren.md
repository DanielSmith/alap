This is from memory — I didn't get a photo and the audio recording clipped
at three seconds. The sighting itself wasn't in doubt; the wren sat on the
kitchen-window perch for almost a minute.
{{< /caution >}}

The Carolina wrens have been pushing further north every year and finally
hit the property on the 19th. Loud for their size — three "teakettle"
phrases right outside the window.

{{< note >}}
The Carolina wren is a fairly large bird, measuring 12.5 to 14 centimeters
in length. It displays rich brown coloring on the crown that becomes more
chestnut-toned on the rump, along with a prominent white stripe above the
eye bordered in black. The throat is gray, transitioning to buff on the
chest and belly.
{{< /note >}}

Males sing year-round with a repertoire of at least twenty different
phrase patterns, often repeating one pattern for several minutes. The
famous "teakettle" song — transcribed by various observers as variations
like *whee-udel* or *túrtee-túrtee-túrtee* — is loud and unmistakable,
making these wrens far more often heard than seen despite their relatively
secretive nature in dense cover.
