## On Bach

Bach's career trajectory took him from provincial German church
positions to eventual recognition as a master musician. Beginning
in 1703 with his appointment as organist in Arnstadt, he moved
through posts in Weimar, Köthen, and finally Leipzig, where he
served as Thomaskantor from 1723 until his death. His early years
involved studying the works of older composers and absorbing
influences from Italian and French musical styles, particularly the
rhythmic dynamism of Vivaldi. By the time he reached Leipzig, Bach
had developed into a composer of extraordinary versatility,
producing sacred cantatas, instrumental works, and theoretical
compositions that synthesized everything he had learned.

Bach's mastery of counterpoint — the art of combining independent
melodic lines — became his defining achievement and lasting legacy.
He is known for his mastery of counterpoint, as heard in *The
Musical Offering* and *The Art of Fugue*, representing the peak of
this technical accomplishment. His approach integrated these
intricate compositional techniques into works of profound
expressive depth, creating music that balanced mathematical
precision with emotional resonance. Though he remained primarily
known as an organist during his lifetime, subsequent generations
recognized him as one of history's greatest composers.
